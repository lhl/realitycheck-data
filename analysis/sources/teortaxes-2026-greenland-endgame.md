## Stage 3: Dialectical Analysis

### Steelmanned Argument
Recast into a version that can be argued with evidence:
- US policy (at least under some administrations) is becoming more openly **transactional/coercive** toward allies, and Greenland is a salient flashpoint.
- **Critical minerals and supply-chain security** are increasingly central to great-power competition; Greenland’s mineral potential makes it strategically legible.
- Europe’s feasible near-term response is **hedging** (symbolic signaling, buying time, selective diversification) rather than direct confrontation.

### Strongest Counterarguments
1. **Motives vs tactics**: Resource-focused bargaining can arise from short-horizon domestic politics, opportunism, or standard great-power bargaining; it does not require “light cone” ideology.
2. **Trade persists under automation**: Even with abundant manufacturing, trade can remain valuable for risk diversification, specialization, and political coalition management; “end trade” is not obviously optimal.
3. **Extraction is the bottleneck**: Having nominal territorial control does not guarantee usable resource flows (permits, infrastructure, refining capacity, capital, local resistance).
4. **Overstatement of actor unity**: US policy is fragmented; elite factions compete (aligns with existing GOV-2026-005).

### Supporting Theories
- `DIST-2026-001`: Compute/energy/chips as chokepoints (consistent with the post’s bottleneck framing).
- `RESOURCE-2026-002`: Vertical integration into raw materials (industrial analog at firm level).

### Contradicting Theories
- `GOV-2026-005`: Inter-elite + interstate competition prevents unified oligarchic coalition (undermines a single coherent maximizer strategy).
- `GOV-2026-004`: Buying stability can be cheaper than enforcing it (undermines “strip assets regardless of alliance legitimacy” as stable equilibrium).

### Synthesis Notes
This source is best treated as a high-voltage narrative that proposes a unifying motive (“light cone” maximization) for a cluster of bargaining moves. Its value is in generating falsifiable sub-questions (what bottlenecks matter; what policies are enacted; what elites actually say), not in its motive attributions.
